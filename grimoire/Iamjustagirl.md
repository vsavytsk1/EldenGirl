# The Curse, the Counter-Curse, and the Engine of Fear: A Global History of Maleficium and Its Remedies

## TL;DR
- The historical evidence strongly supports the core thesis: across cultures, the *fear* of a curse — not any occult mechanism — was the destructive force, and the **counter-magic** (unwitching, diagnosis, naming the culprit) was frequently far more lethal than the alleged curse itself, because a curse is a rumour while a counter-curse requires identifying a living human being to blame.
- The recurring lethal pattern is remarkably stable from Tudor Essex to modern Papua New Guinea: misfortune → demand for explanation → retrospective linking to a marginal person's "muttered" words → violence framed as *self-defence* or *remedy*. Cursing was often the only weapon of the powerless, which is precisely why possessing a reputation for it was so dangerous.
- Physiologically, curses can "work" only through belief: the nocebo effect is well-demonstrated for symptoms and some physiological measures, while "voodoo death" (Cannon 1942, Richter 1957) remains plausible but anecdotal and contested. The greater documented killer has always been the human response to fear — the witch-finder, the mob, the scratching, the pyre.

## Key Findings
- **The curse as legally-recognised evidence.** In many early-modern jurisdictions, a curse followed by misfortune was treated as proof of witchcraft. This inverted the moral order: the muttering beggar became the aggressor and the person who had refused her charity became the "victim," laundering guilt into accusation.
- **Counter-magic as the true instrument of death.** Witch-bottles, apotropaic marks, concealed shoes and cats were passive defences, but the aggressive remedies — "scratching the witch" to draw blood, burning thatch, boiling a bewitched animal's pinned heart to force the witch to appear, and above all consulting a cunning-man who *named* a neighbour — repeatedly converted diffuse anxiety into a targeted human victim.
- **The cunning-folk paradox.** The professional counter-magicians (English cunning-folk, French *devins-guérisseurs*, German *Hexenbanner*) practised magic openly for fees yet were rarely prosecuted, while the poor women they fingered were hanged. The remedy-sellers monetised the fear that killed their targets.
- **The evil eye and inequality.** The single most widespread curse belief encodes a theory of envy and social levelling: praise and admiration are dangerous, prosperity must be concealed, and the countermeasures (blue beads, hamsa, cornicello, egg-cleansing) manage the anxiety of the "limited good."
- **Fear as the engine of atrocity.** Elite demonological fear plus popular neighbourly fear, joined by the confession-under-torture feedback loop, manufactured evidence of a vast conspiracy. Fear was monetised by witch-finders like Matthew Hopkins and remains monetised today by "witch-detector" pastors in Kinshasa.
- **Modern invention and mistranslation.** The "voodoo doll" is largely a European poppet mislabelled by racist stereotype; the Tutankhamun "curse" was a press confection; and many non-European systems are *explanations for misfortune*, not aggression — "curse" is often a colonial mistranslation.

## Details

### 1. The Ancient Archaeology of Cursing

The written curse is one of humanity's oldest surviving genres of private text. The Greek *katadesmoi* and Latin *defixiones* — "binding spells" — were typically inscribed on thin sheets of lead, sometimes rolled up and pierced with a nail, then deposited in graves, wells, springs or chthonic sanctuaries so that underworld powers would carry the binding to its target. Scholars taxonomise their purposes into a handful of recurring anxieties: **litigation curses** (binding an opponent's tongue and memory before a court case — "may he botch his performance, forget his words, become dizzy"), **agonistic/competition curses** against rival athletes and charioteers, **erotic binding spells**, **commercial/trade rivalry** curses, and a distinct category that the Dutch historian **Henk Versnel** separated out as **"prayers for justice."**

Versnel's distinction, proposed in his contribution to *Magika Hiera* (1991) and building on work from the mid-1980s, is crucial to this theme. A classic binding curse is an act of private aggression that coerces a victim regardless of guilt; a "prayer for justice" instead appeals to a deity to punish an *unknown wrongdoer* and restore stolen property, and is framed as legitimate redress. Some scholars refuse to call these "curses" at all.

The British finds show this vividly. The **Bath curse tablets** — about 130 lead and pewter sheets recovered in 1979–1980 from the sacred spring of Sulis Minerva at Aquae Sulis, dating from the 2nd to the late 4th century AD and now on the UNESCO Memory of the World UK Register — are overwhelmingly "prayers for justice" concerning petty theft. One Docilianus asks "the most holy goddess Sulis" to curse "him who has stolen my hooded cloak, whether man or woman, whether slave or free." All but one of the 130 concern the restitution of stolen goods — cloaks, gloves, bathing tunics, small sums of money — the ordinary anxieties of bathers whose belongings were pilfered while they soaked. The nearby **Uley** tablets (from the temple of Mercury) are the other great British corpus. Here the curse is not the weapon of a witch but the recourse of an ordinary victim with no other means of redress — supernatural law enforcement for the powerless.

The deep background is older still. Egyptian **execration texts** (Proscription Lists), attested from the late Old Kingdom through the New Kingdom, inscribed the names of enemies — foreign rulers, ethnic groups, domestic rebels, hostile ghosts — on **red pottery bowls and clay figurines**, which were then ritually **smashed, pierced, burned or buried** to break the enemy's power; over a thousand such deposits are known. Red symbolised danger and the menacing god Set. Biblical scholars (Aage Bentzen, and after him William Holladay and Leo Perdue) have linked the smashing ritual to Jeremiah 19, where the prophet shatters a potter's flask to signify Jerusalem's destruction.

The Near Eastern and biblical traditions institutionalised the curse. The **covenant curses of Deuteronomy 28** (and Leviticus 26) threaten confusion, consumption, inflammation, madness, blindness, military defeat and cannibalism upon covenant-breakers — part of a stock of formulaic maledictions shared across the ancient Near East. The **"bitter water" ordeal of Numbers 5:11–31** subjected a wife suspected of adultery to a priest-administered trial by ordeal — a ritualised, legally-sanctioned curse. The **imprecatory Psalms** and the practice of *herem* (the ban/devotion to destruction) complete the picture: cursing embedded in law and liturgy.

In the Celtic and Irish world, destructive speech was a *recognised profession*. The **áer** (satire) of the *filidh* — poets ranked second only to the king — was so feared that unjust satire entitled the victim to the same compensation as a murder. The **glám dícenn** ("blistering satire") was believed to raise physical blisters on the target's face. Irish law divided poetry into *find* (praise), *dubh* (satire) and *brecc* (a mixed warning, *trefocal*). Beyond the professional poets, the **widow's curse** and the curse of the wronged were greatly feared into the twentieth century; cursing beggar-women let their hair down to signal they were "ready to curse" if refused, and the **cursing stones** (bullaun or "turning stones") of Ireland were turned anticlockwise while a curse was pronounced. Cases persisted into the 1940s–60s. In Norse society, **níð** (ritual insult) and the **níðstang** (the "scorn-pole," a horse's head mounted on a pole and directed at an enemy) were formalised, socially legible acts of aggression.

The theme that unifies all this is that **cursing was often the weapon of the powerless and, in many contexts, socially or legally legitimate.** The "cursing beggar" wielded the one power the destitute possessed: the ability to make the comfortable afraid. This is exactly why a reputation for cursing was double-edged: it offered protection and leverage, but it also marked its holder for destruction.

### 2. The Anatomy of the Curse in Early-Modern Europe — How a Curse Became a Corpse

This is the crux of the argument, and the historiography is unusually clear about the mechanism. The **"charity-refused" model**, formulated by **Keith Thomas** (*Religion and the Decline of Magic*, 1971) and **Alan Macfarlane** (*Witchcraft in Tudor and Stuart England*, 1970), identifies a stable social script. Against a background of rising population, economic hardship and a new individualism that was eroding traditional neighbourly obligation, a poor and often old woman comes to the door asking for alms, milk, food or the loan of a tool. She is refused. She leaves muttering, grumbling, or explicitly cursing. Some later misfortune — a dead cow, a sick child, spoiled butter, a failed brew, a lame horse — is retrospectively linked to her words. Crucially, the refuser's own **guilt** at having broken the Christian duty of charity converts into **anger and then accusation**: the witch becomes the aggressor, and the accuser recovers the moral high ground.

Thomas put it exactly: "The conflict between resentment and a sense of obligation produced the ambivalence which made it possible for men to turn begging women brusquely from the door, and yet suffer torments of conscience after having done so. This ensuing guilt was fertile ground for witchcraft accusations." The 1582 St Osyth case of **Ursula (Ursley) Kemp** is the textbook example: refused money and then cheese by Grace Thurlow, Kemp "swore she would be even with her," Thurlow was taken lame, and Kemp — who insisted she could "unwitch" but "not witche" — was hanged.

The model has been heavily qualified and must be presented with its critiques. The poor appear disproportionately among the accused partly *because they were the largest social group*; not all accused were poorer than their accusers (Malcolm Gaskill notes the 1586 Kent case of Joan Cason, probably wealthier than her accuser; Scottish records list some nobility); and recent work (e.g. Bronach Carter's 2025 study in *Gender & History*) finds that charity-refusal, while common, was not a *necessary* ingredient — in some cases the householder actually gave the suspect what she wanted and the accusation followed anyway. What survives robustly is the **retrospective causal reasoning**: a curse plus a subsequent misfortune equalled evidence.

That evidential point is the legal hinge. Women's speech itself was policed and criminalised: the **"scold"** (the *communis rixatrix*, always gendered female) could be punished with the **cucking or ducking stool** and the **scold's bridle** or **branks** (an iron muzzle with a tongue-plate), and defamation suits over the word "witch" clogged the church courts. A reputation for a "bitter tongue" was, in effect, a standing accusation. In a world where a curse followed by a dead cow was admissible, the muttering woman was perpetually one misfortune away from the gallows.

The agency question is genuinely uncomfortable and deserves emphasis. Some accused women **claimed** the power to curse, or cultivated the reputation as an economic strategy — the "cursing beggar" who was given alms precisely because she was feared. This complicates any tidy story of pure victimhood: the same reputation that extracted a bowl of milk could, after the next misfortune, extract a life. Power and peril were the same coin.

### 3. Counter-Magic and Unwitching — The Arsenal

If the curse was the disease, counter-magic was the cure — and it is here that the fear became lethal. The material record of European counter-witchcraft is vast.

**Witch-bottles** are the signature artefact. Typically a salt-glazed stoneware **Bartmann ("bearded man") or "Bellarmine" jug** — named, misleadingly, for the Counter-Reformation Inquisitor Cardinal Robert Bellarmine (1542–1621), though the jugs predate his notoriety — was filled with the victim's **urine, iron nails, brass pins, hair, fingernail clippings**, and often a **heart-shaped piece of cloth or leather pierced with a bent nail**, then buried inverted beneath the hearth or threshold. The belief was sympathetic and aggressive: the bottle tormented the witch's bladder until she lifted the spell, and if heated in the fire the witch might be killed. The **Greenwich bottle** (found sealed in 2004, studied by Alan Massey of Loughborough and analysed with CT and gas chromatography) is the most complete known example: it contained human urine (from a smoker — cotinine was detected), brimstone, 12 iron nails, 8 brass pins, hair, "navel fluff," a nail-pierced heart-shaped leather, and 10 manicured fingernail clippings (suggesting a person of some social standing). Of roughly 200 known bottles, about 95% of those retaining contents held pins and nails, and around a quarter tested positive for urine. The **Reigate bottle** (1993) similarly confirmed urine and bent pins.

**Apotropaic ("witches'") marks** protected buildings. The most common is the **hexafoil or "daisy wheel"** — a compass-drawn six-petalled flower whose single endless line supposedly trapped and confused evil spirits — followed by **Marian marks**: **VV** ("Virgo Virginum," Virgin of Virgins), **M**, or **AM** (Ave Maria), invoking the Virgin's protection. John Nicholl notes the VV/M practice peaked after James VI/I's *Daemonologie* (1597). **Taper burn marks** (deliberate scorch marks near hearths, to "fight fire with fire") are also widespread. Historic England's 2016 public appeal produced over 600 responses; the largest known concentration is in the caves at **Creswell Crags** on the Nottinghamshire–Derbyshire border, recognised by members of the Subterranea Britannica society. Alongside carved marks, buildings were protected with **ritual concealments**: **concealed shoes** (the earliest recorded English example a pair behind the choir stalls of Winchester Cathedral, installed 1308), **dried cats**, mummified animals, and **horse skulls** under floors and in chimneys.

**Written charms and amulets** carried the same logic in portable form: the **SATOR-AREPO square**, the diminishing **ABRACADABRA triangle**, the **Himmelsbrief** ("Heavenly Letter"), Anglo-Saxon and later metrical charms, and phylacteries. And a whole material vocabulary of protection — **iron** (horseshoes over the door), **salt**, **rowan and red thread**, **hagstones** (naturally holed stones), and apotropaic plants — hedged the ordinary household against malice.

But the passive defences are not where people died. The **aggressive counter-spells** are. The best-attested is **"scratching the witch"** or **"drawing blood above the breath"**: the belief that scratching the suspected witch's face or arm until she bled would break her spell and restore the bewitched. This was not a metaphor. Suspected women were physically assaulted for it across three centuries. The Oxford *Dictionary of English Folklore* records that at **Stratford-upon-Avon in 1867** a man made "a frightful gash" in a woman's cheek saying "There, you old witch, I can do anything with you now"; and at **Long Compton, Warwickshire, in 1875** an 80-year-old woman, **Ann Tennant**, was killed with a pitchfork by the labourer **James Hayward**, who told his trial that drawing her blood would end her power and that sixteen other parishioners deserved the same. In a Central Criminal Court case, a young man, Charles Tallbrook, cut his own grandmother, Mary King, with a razor solely to "draw her blood" and break her supposed power. Other aggressive remedies included **burning the thatch** from a suspect's roof, and **boiling the bewitched animal's heart stuck with pins** (or heating a horseshoe or a witch-bottle) to inflict pain on the witch and compel her to appear and lift the curse. The **swimming test** (ducking) functioned simultaneously as legal proof and as counter-magic.

Presiding over all of this were the **cunning-folk** — the professional counter-magicians documented by **Owen Davies** (*Popular Magic: Cunning-folk in English History*, 2003) and, on their inner spiritual world, **Emma Wilby** (*Cunning Folk and Familiar Spirits*, 2005). Cunning-folk found lost goods, told fortunes, healed, sold love-magic, and — most dangerously — **diagnosed bewitchment and identified the witch responsible.** They charged fees, worked from grimoires and manuals, and were, as Davies stresses, remarkably *tolerated*: church and state usually distinguished their "service" magic from malevolent witchcraft, so the practitioners of magic went unpunished while the poor women their diagnoses fingered were hanged. This is the sharpest form of the irony — **the licensed magician's counter-curse was the mechanism that killed the unlicensed "witch."** The type persisted across Europe and into the twentieth century: the French **devins-guérisseurs**, the German **Hexenbanner** ("witch-banishers"), the Scandinavian **kloke folk** ("wise folk"), and Italian and Iberian equivalents.

### 4. The Evil Eye

The evil eye is the most globally widespread curse belief, stretching, in Alan Dundes's phrase, "from Ireland to India," and strongest in the Indo-European and Semitic world — the Mediterranean, the Middle East, South Asia, Latin America and their diasporas. Its names map its reach: **malocchio** (Italian), **mati** (Greek), **nazar** (Turkish/Persian/Arabic/Urdu), **ayin hara** (Hebrew), **mal de ojo / ojo** (Spanish), **drishti** and **buri nazar** (South Asia). It is ancient, appearing in Sumerian texts and the Bible.

Its etiology is **envy**, and the anthropological literature ties it tightly to **inequality**. **George Foster's** "image of limited good" (1965) holds that in peasant societies there is presumed to be only a fixed quantum of health, wealth and fortune, so one person's gain is another's loss; visible prosperity therefore invites envious, damaging attention. This is why the evil eye clusters around **praise and admiration** — and why complimenting a baby is taboo, or must be immediately neutralised (touching the child, spitting, or saying "God bless"). **Alan Dundes**, in "Wet and Dry, the Evil Eye" (1981), reads the complex as a fear of the drying-up of vital liquids (milk, semen, water, the moisture of life), explaining why remedies so often involve liquid or spitting, and why the belief is weak in America's "unlimited good" culture. **Clarence Maloney's** edited volume *The Evil Eye* (1976) and Brian Spooner's work map its distribution and critique the pure-envy theory.

The countermeasures are a global folk-technology of reassurance: the Turkish **nazar boncuğu** (blue glass eye-bead, ubiquitous in Turkey and often paired with the phrase "Allah korusun!", "May God protect"); the **hamsa/khamsa** (the five-fingered amulet — the Hand of Fatima in Islam, the Hand of Miriam in Judaism — with roots in the ancient Near East); the Italian **cornicello** (a horn-shaped, traditionally red-coral amulet, described by Venetian jewellers as "the most powerful and traditional Italian amulet against the Evil Eye") and the **mano cornuta** and **mano fica** hand-gestures; **spitting** (three times); and diagnostic-curative rituals such as the Greek reading of oil dropped in water, the Turkish **kurşun dökme** (pouring molten lead over the covered head of the afflicted person into cold water and reading the solidified shapes while reciting Qur'anic verses), and the Mexican **limpia**, in which a *curandera* rubs a room-temperature egg over the body head-to-toe while praying and then cracks it into a glass of water, "reading" cloudy, bloody, or cobweb-like yolk as evidence of *mal de ojo*. Anthropologically, these are best understood as a mechanism for **regulating envy and levelling social difference** in face-to-face agrarian societies — a way of managing the dangerous emotions that inequality produces.

### 5. Global and Non-European Cursing and Counter-Cursing

The comparative record confirms that the deep logic — misfortune demands a human cause, and the *identification* of that cause is where the violence lives — is close to universal, but it must be handled with care, because in many of these systems "curse" is a colonial mistranslation and the practice is primarily an **explanation for misfortune**, not an act of aggression.

The foundational text is **E. E. Evans-Pritchard's** *Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic Among the Azande* (1937), based on 1926–1930 fieldwork in the southern Sudan. The Azande distinguish **mangu** (witchcraft — an inherited bodily substance that can harm others *unconsciously*, without ritual) from **ngua** (sorcery — deliberate bad medicine). Evans-Pritchard's famous **granary example** captures the whole intellectual system: when a granary, undermined by termites, collapses and kills the people sheltering beneath it, the Azande do not deny that termites weakened it — they ask *why it fell at that particular moment on those particular people*, and witchcraft answers that question of coincidence and meaning. Guilt is determined by the **benge poison oracle**: poison is fed to a chicken while questions are posed, and the bird's death or survival answers. Critically, the accused witch, once named, typically professes innocence of any conscious intent and performs a rite blowing water to "cool" his *mangu* — a comparatively *non-lethal* resolution. Evans-Pritchard read the whole system as both a theory of misfortune and a social safety-valve.

Other traditions, surveyed with respect:
- **African juju/muti**: harm-magic and, in its darkest modern form, "muti murders" for body parts believed to empower medicine.
- **Haitian Vodou and Louisiana Hoodoo/Voodoo**: complex Afro-diasporic religions of healing, protection and communal care, grievously distorted by the racist **"voodoo doll"** stereotype. As folklorists and practitioners stress, the pin-stuck doll is **not** a feature of Haitian Vodou; it derives principally from the **European poppet** tradition of image-magic, conflated by Hollywood and pulp fiction (the term "voodoo doll" gained currency from the 1930s) with the Central African **nkisi** (nail-studded power-figures used to *provoke* a spirit for healing or justice). The "voodoo doll" is, as one summary puts it, "a brand, not an anthropological category."
- **European poppets/"mommets"**: genuine image-magic, and here the curse *did* reach kings. In the North Berwick witch trials (1590–91), **Agnes Sampson** and others were accused of conspiring with the Earl of Bothwell to kill James VI, including by melting a wax image; image-magic against monarchs was a recurring treasonous fear.
- **Melanesian sorcery**: Trobriand **bwaga'u** (male sorcerers) and **mulukwausi** (flying witches), documented by **Bronislaw Malinowski**, who attributed nearly all serious illness and death to sorcery and grounded magical power in the enunciated word; PNG **sanguma**; and the "death-pointing" **vada**.
- **Aboriginal Australian "pointing the bone" / kurdaitcha**: among the Arrernte of Central Australia, first documented by Baldwin Spencer and F. J. Gillen (*The Native Tribes of Central Australia*, 1899), a ritual executioner points a charmed bone (*kundela*) at a victim who, believing himself doomed, may sicken and die — the paradigm case of death by belief, discussed below.
- **Native American traditions**, handled with the reticence they demand: **Clyde Kluckhohn's** *Navaho Witchcraft* (1944), based on 93 informants, defines witchcraft as "the influencing of events by super-natural techniques that are socially disapproved," documents the profound Navajo taboo on even *discussing* witchcraft, and reads the belief as a socially-channelled outlet for anxiety and hostility.
- **South Asia**: **jadu-tona** and **tantra-mantra** (harmful magic) and the protective **nazar utarna** (removing the evil eye).
- **China**: **gu** (蠱) poison-sorcery, made by sealing venomous creatures together until one cannibal survivor concentrates the toxin — the character attested in Shang oracle-bone script.
- **Japan**: **ushi no koku mairi**, the "shrine visit at the hour of the ox" (1–3 a.m.), in which a scorned woman in white, wearing an iron crown of three candles, hammers a straw effigy (*wara ningyō*) to a sacred tree.
- **Southeast Asia/Philippines**: **kulam** and **barang** (sorcery causing illness via foreign objects magically introduced into the body).

### 6. The Psychology and Physiology of Fear — Does a Curse "Work"?

Here the honest answer is layered, and the guiding instinct — that fear is the mechanism — is essentially correct, but the strength of evidence varies sharply by claim.

The classic starting point is the Harvard physiologist **Walter B. Cannon's** 1942 paper **"'Voodoo' Death"** (*American Anthropologist* 44:169–181), which collected anecdotal reports — from Australia, Africa, New Zealand, South America — of people who, believing themselves cursed or having broken a fatal taboo, declined and died within hours or days. Cannon proposed a **sympathetic–adrenal** mechanism: overwhelming, sustained fear triggers a massive catecholamine (adrenaline) release, causing vasoconstriction, a drop in effective blood volume, circulatory shock and death. The essential precondition, in every case Cannon cited, was the victim's **absolute belief** that an external power could kill at will and that he was helpless against it.

**Curt Richter's** 1957 paper "On the Phenomenon of Sudden Death in Animals and Man" (*Psychosomatic Medicine* 19:191–198) explicitly extended Cannon's work and pointed in a different physiological direction. Richter's grim experiments showed domesticated rats swimming far longer than wild rats, which sometimes died within minutes of immersion; and crucially, that briefly rescuing rats before returning them to the water abolished the sudden deaths — "after elimination of hopelessness the rats do not die." Richter, noting the heart slowed (rather than raced) before death, argued the deaths reflected **hopelessness** and a **parasympathetic/vagal** mechanism, not sympathetic overdrive — the opposite of Cannon. This tension has never been fully resolved.

Modern reassessments (Barbara Lex; **Esther Sternberg's** 2002 *American Journal of Public Health* perspective marking Cannon's paper "60 years on"; and neurocardiologists such as Martin Samuels) reconcile the two by invoking **catecholamine toxicity** and its downstream cardiac effects. The most plausible modern physiological mechanism is **takotsubo cardiomyopathy** ("broken-heart syndrome," "stress cardiomyopathy"), in which a surge of stress hormones stuns the left ventricle, producing a genuine, sometimes fatal, acute cardiac event triggered by pure emotional shock. This gives "voodoo death" a credible modern pathway — while leaving the ethnographic cases themselves anecdotal and unverifiable.

The best-*evidenced* mechanism is the **nocebo effect** — the evil twin of the placebo — in which negative expectation produces real harm. The clinical literature (Fabrizio Benedetti, Luana Colloca, Winfried Häuser and colleagues) documents that verbal suggestion, prior experience, observational learning and even reading a drug leaflet can generate genuine adverse symptoms, measurable pain (hyperalgesia via cholecystokinin pathways) and physiological changes; that disclosing side-effects in informed consent can *cause* those side-effects; and that patients in countries with more online statin-warning content show more statin intolerance. Colloca's 2024 *Annual Review of Pharmacology and Toxicology* review synthesises the field. The nocebo effect is the scientifically respectable core of the curse: it is demonstrably true that **being told, and believing, that you have been harmed can harm you** — chiefly at the level of symptoms, though the leap from symptom to death remains unproven and the widely-cited "Mr A" antidepressant-overdose case (a man whose placebo "overdose" produced dangerously low blood pressure that reversed on being told the truth) is contested.

Belief operates at scale, too. **Mass psychogenic illness** (the spread of symptoms with no organic cause through a frightened population) and the sociology of fear contagion — **Stanley Cohen's** moral-panic theory, **Jean-Noël Kapferer's** work on rumour — explain how fear metastasises. This bears directly on the trial-record "hysterical" symptoms: the afflicted girls of Salem, and the convent possessions at **Loudun** and **Louviers**. The popular **ergotism hypothesis** for Salem (Linnda Caporael, "Ergotism: The Satan Loosed in Salem?", *Science*, 1976) was decisively rebutted within months by **Nicholas Spanos and Jack Gottlieb** ("Ergotism and the Salem Village Witch Trials," *Science* 194:1390–1394, December 1976): convulsive ergotism requires severe vitamin-A deficiency absent in dairy-and-fish-rich Salem; whole families sharing the same rye did not fall ill together; and the symptoms and the crisis's abrupt end fit psychogenic and social explanations far better. Most Salem historians now reject the moldy-bread theory.

### 7. Fear as the Engine of Atrocity

The European witch-hunts were driven by the convergence of two fears. **Elite fear** was theological and conspiratorial: the demonological theory (crystallised in the *Malleus Maleficarum*, 1486, and later works) held that witches formed a vast satanic conspiracy, sealed by a pact and the sabbath. **Popular fear** was local and material: the neighbour's malice, the muttered curse, the dead cow. Where the two met, catastrophe followed — and the mechanism that fused them and manufactured "proof" of the grand conspiracy was **torture**.

The Jesuit **Friedrich Spee von Langenfeld**, who had served as confessor to condemned witches, exposed the machinery in ***Cautio Criminalis*** (1631, published pseudonymously). His devastating argument was that torture created a closed logical trap: it produced not only a confession but **denunciations of "accomplices,"** who were then arrested and tortured into naming others, "spreading the circle of torture and execution ever wider." There was, Spee showed, **no way for an innocent person to prove innocence** — the system generated its own escalating evidence of a conspiracy that did not exist. This is the feedback loop that turned local fear into mass death, and it is the intellectual key to the whole phenomenon.

Fear was also **monetised**. In England, **Matthew Hopkins**, styling himself "Witchfinder General" (a title Parliament never granted), and his associate **John Stearne** conducted the largest witch-hunt in English history across East Anglia in 1644–1647, exploiting the collapse of normal judicial order during the Civil War. Beginning at Manningtree with the accusation of the widow **Elizabeth Clarke**, and using sleep-deprivation, "watching" and the search for the Devil's mark, the pair are estimated by Malcolm Gaskill and others to have sent roughly 200 women plus some 17–20 men to the gallows — around 60% of all English witch executions — with the total number of accused often given as more than 300. Hopkins charged towns for his services. On **27 August 1645** at Bury St Edmunds, 18 men and women were hanged together in what has been called "the single biggest mass-execution for witchcraft in English history"; the parliamentary news-sheet *The Moderate Intelligencer* began to question why the Devil would communicate only with "poor, uneducated women." Comparable fear-entrepreneurs operated elsewhere: German *Hexenkommissare*, the Scottish professional "prickers" (who searched for insensible marks), and prince-bishops' agents like **Balthasar Voss (Ross)** at Fulda. The **economics of fear** — confiscation of the accused's estate, fees per conviction, court costs charged to victims' families — gave officials a direct financial stake in guilt.

The hunts ended not because of "science" but because of **legal-evidential scepticism**. Reginald Scot's *The Discoverie of Witchcraft* (1584) had denied witches' powers outright; **Johann Weyer** (*De praestigiis daemonum*, 1563) argued the accused were deluded or ill; in Spain, the Inquisition's own **Alonso de Salazar Frías** — the "Witches' Advocate" — conducted a 1611–1612 visitation after the Logroño auto-da-fé of 1610 under an Edict of Grace, documenting nearly 7,000 people named (1,802 confessions, including 1,384 from children aged 6–14, plus some 5,000 further accusations) yet finding *zero* instances of physical evidence, and persuaded the Suprema to adopt almost all his restraining recommendations in its 1614 instructions — effectively ending Spanish witch persecution, on his conclusion that the panic itself created the illusions. The jurist **Christian Thomasius** at Halle attacked the trials' legal basis around 1701. The endpoint in Britain was the **Witchcraft Act 1735**, which repealed the older statutes and — the decisive conceptual reversal — made it an offence not to *practise* witchcraft but to **pretend to**: henceforth the law punished those who "pretend to exercise or use any kind of Witchcraft, Sorcery, Inchantment, or Conjuration," treating the whole thing as fraud upon "ignorant persons." Europe's last executions came late: **Janet Horne** at Dornoch in Scotland (1727), and **Anna Göldi**, beheaded at Glarus, Switzerland, in **1782** — often called the last witch executed in Europe, and posthumously exonerated by the Glarus parliament in 2008.

The most sobering finding is that **the fear-machinery still operates.** Contemporary witchcraft accusations kill thousands. In **Tanzania**, the Legal and Human Rights Centre (LHRC) recorded 765 people killed over witchcraft accusations in 2013 (505 of them women) and 630 in 2012, and reported 479 women killed in the first six months of 2017 alone — many of them elderly women (with red eyes from smoky cooking fires), alongside the separate murders of **people with albinism** for body parts. In **Papua New Guinea**, sorcery-accusation-related violence (against alleged *sanguma*) prompted the 2013 repeal of the 1971 Sorcery Act and its replacement with wilful-murder provisions. In **India**, the National Crime Records Bureau recorded more than 2,500 people — mainly women — killed over accusations of witchcraft or black magic between 2000 and 2016 (averaging roughly 150 deaths a year), with 85 witchcraft-related murders recorded in 2022 alone; killings are concentrated in states such as Jharkhand and Odisha. In the **Democratic Republic of Congo**, UNICEF's 2010 report *Children Accused of Witchcraft* cited an estimate that more than 20,000 children were living on the streets of Kinshasa after being accused of witchcraft and abandoned — many "diagnosed" by Pentecostal pastors who then charge for deliverance. It was this continuing atrocity that produced the **UN Human Rights Council resolution 47/8** — "Elimination of harmful practices related to accusations of witchcraft and ritual attacks," adopted without a vote on 12 July 2021 — providing continuity with the prior report's coverage.

In the West, the same structure appears as **moral panic**. The **Satanic Ritual Abuse panic** of the 1980s–90s — ignited by the 1980 book *Michelle Remembers* (psychiatrist Lawrence Pazder and his patient, later wife, Michelle Smith), built on the now-discredited technique of **recovered-memory therapy**, and propelled through cases like the **McMartin Preschool trial** (one of the longest and costliest in US history, ending in no convictions) — reproduced the witch-hunt template exactly: a terrifying hidden conspiracy, coerced testimony, "experts" who monetised fear, and the destruction of innocent lives. Elizabeth Loftus's memory research and the collapse of the cases discredited the panic, but its descendants recur in modern conspiracy movements.

### 8. Legacy and Pop Culture

The curse survives as a narrative form because it satisfies deep cognitive appetites. The **curse of Tutankhamun** is the exemplary modern fabrication. When Lord Carnarvon died of an infected mosquito bite in Cairo on 5 April 1923, weeks after the burial chamber was opened, the press — inflamed by *The Times*'s exclusive deal, which had frozen out rivals like the Egyptologist-journalist **Arthur Weigall** — manufactured a "curse." The best-selling novelist **Marie Corelli** had already primed the public with a warning (in *The Express*, 24 March 1923) that, according to a rare book she possessed, "the most dire punishment follows any rash intruder into a sealed tomb," and **Sir Arthur Conan Doyle**, a devout Spiritualist, suggested an "evil elemental" created by Tutankhamun's priests. No curse text was ever found in the tomb. A **2002 *British Medical Journal* study by Mark Nelson** (Monash University) examined the 44 Westerners Carter recorded as present in Egypt and found **no significant difference in survival** between the 25 "exposed" to the tomb (mean age at death 70) and those not exposed (mean age at death 75) — concluding there was "no evidence to support the existence of a mummy's curse."

The pattern repeats in the **Hope Diamond**, the **Kennedy curse**, and sporting "curses" — the Boston Red Sox's **Curse of the Bambino** and the Chicago Cubs' **Billy Goat** — each a post-hoc story imposed on ordinary runs of bad luck. The **"Macbeth" / "Scottish Play"** theatre superstition (never speak the name inside a theatre) shows the same reasoning applied to an art form, as do modern **chain letters** and their digital descendants (the "forward this or else" message). And apotropaic behaviour is still ubiquitous and unremarked: **knocking on wood**, the Italian **tocca ferro** ("touch iron"), saying **"bless you"** after a sneeze, and **spitting three times** are living fragments of the counter-curse.

Why are curse narratives so "sticky"? Several well-established cognitive biases converge: the **just-world hypothesis** (misfortune must be deserved, so we seek a moral cause); **hyperactive agency detection** (we are primed to see intention behind events); **illusory correlation** (linking the curse and the calamity because both are salient); and **post-hoc causal reasoning** (*post hoc ergo propter hoc* — the curse came first, so it must be the cause). These are the very same mechanisms that, in Tudor Essex or modern Jharkhand, turn a dead cow into a dead woman. The curse is cognitively natural; that is exactly what makes it dangerous.

## Recommendations
For a reader or writer developing this angle further, the evidence supports a staged approach:

1. **Lead with the mechanism, not the exotica.** The strongest, most defensible thesis is structural: *fear seeking a cause* + *the demand to name a human culprit* = atrocity. Foreground the charity-refused script (Thomas/Macfarlane) and Spee's torture feedback loop as the twin engines, then use the global cases (Azande, kurdaitcha, PNG) as confirmation of a near-universal logic — not as a freak-show.

2. **Keep the counter-curse irony central and concrete.** The single most original through-line is that the *remedy* killed more reliably than the *curse*. Anchor it in named cases: Ann Tennant (Long Compton, 1875), Ursula Kemp (St Osyth, 1582), the cunning-folk who named victims for a fee, and the modern Kinshasa pastors who "diagnose" child witches for payment.

3. **Calibrate the science carefully.** State plainly what is demonstrated (nocebo effects on symptoms and some physiological measures; takotsubo as a plausible pathway) versus what is anecdotal/contested (death purely by curse; Cannon's cases; the "Mr A" report). Treat Richter's hopelessness model and the sympathetic/parasympathetic debate as unresolved. Do not overclaim.

4. **Flag the fabrications explicitly.** The "voodoo doll," the Tutankhamun curse, and the Salem ergotism theory should each be presented as modern inventions or debunked hypotheses, with the debunking sources named (Nelson 2002; Spanos & Gottlieb 1976).

5. **Handle non-European and living traditions with anthropological respect.** Use practitioners' and scholars' own terms; stress that most systems are *explanations for misfortune*, not aggression; and note where "curse" is a colonial mistranslation. Connect explicitly to the ongoing human-rights emergency (UNHRC 47/8) rather than leaving contemporary killings as a curiosity.

**Benchmarks that would change these conclusions:** a rigorous, prospectively-documented case of death attributable to belief alone (absent poisoning, dehydration or pre-existing disease) would strengthen the "voodoo death" claim from plausible to demonstrated; conversely, replicated failure to find nocebo effects on hard physiological endpoints would confine the curse's power to subjective symptom-reporting.

## Caveats
- **The "charity-refused" model is a strong tendency, not a law.** It has been substantially qualified: the poor dominate the accused partly because they were the majority; not all accusers outranked the accused; and charity-refusal was frequent but not necessary. Present it as one powerful pattern among several.
- **"Voodoo death" is real as a hypothesis, unproven as a fact.** The ethnographic cases are anecdotal, often second-hand, and collected by observers with their own assumptions. Takotsubo and nocebo give credible partial mechanisms, but a clean causal demonstration in humans does not exist.
- **Numbers for both historical and contemporary killings are estimates.** English execution totals, Hopkins's body-count (~100+ by some counts, up to ~200–300 by others), and modern figures from Tanzania (LHRC), India (NCRB) and the DRC (UNICEF) come from advocacy groups, journalism and rapporteurs; governments rarely publish official counts and underreporting is acknowledged. Treat all such figures as best-available approximations, not precise tallies.
- **Some sources encountered are low-authority.** Occult-practitioner blogs, content-farm pages and AI-generated encyclopaedias circulate around these topics; this report relies where possible on named scholars (Thomas, Macfarlane, Davies, Wilby, Evans-Pritchard, Versnel, Dundes, Foster, Kluckhohn, Malinowski, Gaskill, Henningsen), peer-reviewed journals (*Science*, *BMJ*, *American Anthropologist*, *Psychosomatic Medicine*, *Annual Review of Pharmacology and Toxicology*) and institutional sources (Historic England, UN OHCHR).
- **Cultural sensitivity.** Living traditions (Vodou, Hoodoo, Navajo, Aboriginal Australian, sanguma) are described as documented ethnographic phenomena, not endorsed or operationalised; several have been gravely harmed by the very stereotypes this report analyses. This is scholarship on belief and its consequences, not a manual.
