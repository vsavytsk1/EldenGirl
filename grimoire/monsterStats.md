# The True Scale of Violence Against Women: Prevalence, Femicide, and the Reporting Gap

## TL;DR
- Roughly 1 in 3 women worldwide — around 736 million (WHO 2018 estimates, published 2021) — have experienced physical and/or sexual intimate partner violence (IPV) or non-partner sexual violence in their lifetime; 27% of ever-partnered women aged 15–49 (uncertainty interval 23–31%) report lifetime IPV. Yet fewer than 40% of women who experience violence seek help of any sort, and of those who do, fewer than 10% go to police — so official statistics capture only a small fraction of reality.
- Femicide is the lethal tip: UNODC/UN Women estimate ~50,000 women and girls were killed by intimate partners or family members in 2024 (60% of all female intentional killings) — an average of 137 per day, roughly one every 10 minutes — versus just 11% of male homicide victims killed by partners/family.
- The justice funnel collapses at every stage: in England & Wales fewer than 1 in 6 (16%) rape victims report to police, and of 71,227 rapes recorded in the year ending December 2024, only about 2.7% led to a charge that same year — a vivid, citable illustration of the "dark figure" of gender-based crime.

## Key Findings

### 1. Global prevalence (WHO Violence Against Women Prevalence Estimates, 2018, published March/May 2021)
- **Headline:** 27% (uncertainty interval [UI] 23–31%) of ever-partnered women aged 15–49 have experienced physical and/or sexual IPV in their lifetime; 13% (UI 10–16%) in the past 12 months. For women aged 15 and older, lifetime prevalence is 26% (UI 22–30%) and past-year is 10% (UI 8–12%).
- **Absolute number:** Around 736 million women have experienced IPV and/or non-partner sexual violence at least once in their lives — "a number that has remained largely unchanged over the past decade" (WHO/PAHO). IPV alone is the most prevalent form, affecting about 641 million.
- **Young women — the "starts early" finding:** 24% (UI 21–28%) of women aged 15–19 and 26% (UI 23–30%) aged 20–24 have already experienced IPV; WHO frames this as 1 in 4 young women (15–24) who have been in a relationship experiencing IPV by their mid-twenties. Past-year prevalence peaks at 16% in both the 15–19 and 20–24 brackets and declines with age (to ~4% at 65+).
- **Non-partner sexual violence:** 6% globally (95% UI 4–9%), about 1 in 17 women aged 15–49 — based on data from 137 countries and widely regarded as a gross underestimate due to stigma.
- **Regional variation (WHO regions, lifetime IPV):** highest in the WHO African Region and South-East Asia Region (~33% each), Eastern Mediterranean (31%), Region of the Americas (25%), European Region (~22–25%), and Western Pacific Region (lowest, ~20%). By UN SDG subregion, prevalence reaches 51% in Melanesia (Oceania), 41% Micronesia, 39% Polynesia, 35% Southern Asia, 33% sub-Saharan Africa; Southern Europe is lowest at 16%.
- **Methodology:** 366 eligible studies capturing ~2 million women's responses, from 161 countries and areas (90% of the global female population aged 15+), 2000–2018, using a Bayesian multilevel model with act-based measures. Produced for the UN Inter-Agency Working Group (VAW-IAWGED).

### 2. Femicide and intimate-partner homicide (UNODC/UN Women annual "Femicides" reports)
- **2024 data (published 24–25 Nov 2025):** ~83,000 women and girls killed intentionally; 60% — about 50,000 — by intimate partners or family members; an average of 137 per day, roughly one every 10 minutes.
- **2023 data (published 25 Nov 2024):** 85,000 women/girls killed; 60% (51,000) by intimate partners/family; 140 per day.
- **2022 data (published 22 Nov 2023):** ~89,000 killed — the highest annual figure recorded in two decades — of which 55% (48,800) by partners/family; more than 133 per day.
- **Regional breakdown (2024, per 100,000 female population, and absolute victims):** Africa highest at 3.0 per 100,000 (~22,600 victims); Americas 1.5 (~7,700); Oceania 1.4 (~300); Asia 0.7 (~17,400); Europe 0.5 (~2,100). Africa surpassed Asia in aggregate victims for the first time in 2022. In Europe and the Americas most domestic-sphere killings are by intimate partners (64% and 58%); elsewhere family members predominate.
- **The asymmetry (visualize this):** In 2024, just 11% of male homicide victims were killed by intimate partners/family, versus 60% of female victims. Men are ~81% of all homicide victims but are killed overwhelmingly in the public sphere; women/girls make up ~53% of all victims killed by intimate partners/family. UN Women's older framing: ~47% of female murders are by partner/family versus <6% of male murders.
- **Data collapse:** Only 37 countries reported intimate-partner/family femicide data for 2023, down sharply from 75 in 2020. Gender-related motive is missing in roughly 4 in 10 (about 40%) female homicides, so UNODC totals are lower-bound estimates.

### 3. The reporting gap (the core of this request)
- **UN Women global benchmark:** "Less than 40 per cent of the women who experience violence seek help of any sort… Less than 10 per cent of those seeking help appealed to the police." Most who seek help turn to family and friends, not formal institutions — meaning well under 10% of incidents ever reach police.
- **EU-wide (Eurostat/FRA/EIGE Gender-Based Violence Survey, key results published 25 Nov 2024; 114,023 women interviewed across all 27 Member States):** "1 in 3 women in the EU have experienced physical violence, sexual violence, or threats" in adulthood and "1 in 6 women… have experienced sexual violence, including rape." On reporting: "only 1 in 5 have contacted a healthcare or social service provider, and just 1 in 8 have reported the incident to the police." The statistical report found the number of sexual offences reported to police accounts for **less than 5%** of women who told the survey they had experienced any sexual act of violence. National lifetime prevalence ranged from ~12% (Bulgaria) to ~57% (Finland) — a spread driven as much by reporting culture and awareness as by true prevalence.
- **United States (NCVS / Bureau of Justice Statistics):** BJS calls the NCVS the definitive measure of the "dark figure of crime." In 2022, about 42% of all violent victimizations were reported to police. Reporting of rape/sexual assault is volatile — 46% in 2023 (the highest since 2010) but much lower in typical years and long regarded as seriously undercounted even in the survey itself. About 52% of domestic violence victimizations were reported to police in 2019, down from roughly two-thirds in 2010.
- **England & Wales (survey vs police-recorded):** The Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW) shows fewer than 1 in 6 (16%) victims of rape/assault by penetration report to police (ONS, combining years ending March 2017 and March 2020). CSEW lifetime prevalence (YE March 2025): 25.6% of adult women (~1 in 4) experienced sexual assault since age 16; 7.3% of women experienced rape/attempted rape since 16 (~1.8 million). ONS stresses CSEW is a more reliable trend measure than police-recorded crime.
- **Australia (ABS Personal Safety Survey 2021-22):** 27% of women (2.7 million) have experienced partner violence or abuse since age 15; 22% of women (2.2 million) experienced sexual violence since 15 — and women were over three times more likely to be assaulted by a man they knew than a stranger. Survey prevalence vastly exceeds police records because most incidents are never reported.
- **Methodology point (essential for honest visualization):** Prevalence surveys (WHO, CSEW, ABS PSS, FRA) ask representative population samples about experiences whether or not they were reported; police-recorded crime counts only incidents reported and recorded as crimes. The gap between them is the underreporting or "dark figure." Even surveys undercount, due to stigma, recall, and the safety of the interview setting.

### 4. Attrition through the justice system (England & Wales rape — the best-documented funnel)
No single official source publishes this end-to-end; it is assembled from ONS, Home Office, CPS, and MoJ, which use different units and time periods (a caveat worth showing on the chart):
- **Prevalence:** ~739,000 women experience sexual assault (including attempts) each year (ONS, YE March 2025); ~6.3 million women have experienced it since age 16.
- **Reporting:** fewer than 1 in 6 (16%) rape/penetration victims report to police (ONS/CSEW).
- **Recorded:** 71,227 rapes recorded by police in the year ending December 2024 (up 5% on the prior year); 205,465 sexual offences recorded in total (up 8%).
- **Charged:** approximately 2.7% of recorded rapes (YE December 2024) resulted in a charge/summons that same year — "fewer than 3 in 100." (The Home Office charge rate was 2.6% for YE March 2024 and 2.8% (2,040 charge/summonses) for YE March 2025.) **Important:** charge rates are provisional first-published snapshots and rise as investigations close — revised earlier-year rates reached 3.2–3.9%.
- **Prosecuted and convicted:** CPS adult-rape prosecutions numbered 2,283 with 1,220 convictions in 2023/24, a ~53% conviction rate of completed prosecutions. The independent jury conviction rate for rape (Prof. Cheryl Thomas KC, UCL, using MoJ data on jury verdicts) averaged 58% over 2007–2021, rising to 75% in 2021. The frequently cited "68%" is an older CPS completed-prosecution figure and should **not** be labelled a "jury conviction rate."
- **Time and backlog:** Adult rape cases take on average about two years (~730 days) from report to court completion; police-to-charge alone averages ~306–317 days. MoJ recorded a backlog of ~11,918 outstanding sexual-offence cases (Q4 2024), including ~3,498 adult rape cases.
- **Stated reasons for not reporting (ONS):** embarrassment (40%), did not think the police could help (38%), thought it would be humiliating (34%).

### 5. Coercive control specifically
- **Criminalization:** England & Wales (Serious Crime Act 2015, in force December 2015); Scotland (Domestic Abuse (Scotland) Act 2018); Ireland (Domestic Violence Act 2018, in force 2019); and various Australian states.
- **England & Wales — recorded vs convicted (the attrition is stark):** recorded coercive-control offences rose from ~4,246 (2016-17) to 9,052 (2017-18), 33,954 (YE March 2021), 45,310 (YE March 2024), and **49,557 (YE March 2025)**. Yet there were only 1,299 defendant proceedings and 853 convictions for controlling/coercive behaviour in the year ending December 2024 — of whom 832 of 853 (98%) were male. CPS charges climbed from 198 (2016) to 4,955 (2024). The gap between ~49,557 recorded offences and ~853 convictions dramatizes how poorly non-physical abuse is captured by the justice system; a local analysis found only ~3% of completed coercive-control investigations resulted in a charge.
- **Economic abuse (ABS PSS 2021-22):** ~1 in 6 women (16%) and 1 in 13 men (7.8%) experienced partner economic abuse since 15; 23% of women experienced partner emotional abuse.
- **Technology-facilitated abuse / stalkerware (Kaspersky State of Stalkerware 2023):** 31,031 unique users affected globally, up 5.8% from 29,312 in 2022, across 175 countries. Top affected: Russia (9,890), Brazil (4,186), India (2,492). 195 distinct stalkerware apps detected; TrackView most common (4,049 users). In an accompanying 21,000-person survey (Arlington Research), ~40% reported experiencing or suspecting stalking, and 39% reported violence/abuse from a current or former partner. These detections capture only Kaspersky users — a small fraction of the true total.

### 6. Why women don't report (survey-based reasons)
- **ONS (England & Wales):** embarrassment (40%), didn't think police could help (38%), thought it would be humiliating (34%); many viewed it as a private/personal matter.
- **US NCVS (2005 data):** private/personal matter (22% of women, 39% of men), fear of reprisal (12% women, 5% men), desire to protect the offender (14% women).
- **US advocate survey (ACLU, "Responses from the Field," 900 advocates/service providers):** 88% said police sometimes/often do not believe or blame victims; nearly 90% said police contact sometimes/often triggered child-protective-services involvement, threatening loss of custody; other cited consequences include immigration proceedings and loss of housing, employment, or benefits.
- **Recurrence:** one study found 90% of women previously involved with the criminal legal system did not contact police for some or all subsequent victimizations.
- **Global pattern (WHO multi-country study):** 55–95% of women who experienced physical/sexual IPV had never sought help from formal institutions; formal help-seeking is lowest in low-income, high gender-inequality settings (e.g., ~1–2% seeking formal help in India and Bangladesh).

### 7. Escalation and separation — leaving is the most dangerous period
- **Campbell et al. (2003), 11-city case-control study of intimate partner femicide (220 femicide victims via police/medical-examiner records, 343 abused controls; American Journal of Public Health):** estrangement — "especially from a controlling partner" — is a key preincident risk factor; separation after cohabitation raised risk (odds ratio ≈ 3.6), and the victim having left for another partner was a significant incident factor. The strongest factors were the perpetrator's access to a gun (OR ≈ 7.6) and prior threat with a weapon. This study underpins Campbell's Danger Assessment lethality tool.
- **Timing of elevated risk:** the first 3 months and the first year after separation are the most lethal, with risk declining over time (Campbell 2003/2007). Research indicates nearly all male-to-female intimate partner homicides occur within the first year after separation, with about 50% in the first two months (Wilson & Daly).
- **Proportion separated:** approximately 44% of femicide victims in Campbell's data were separated when killed. The UK Femicide Census found 41% of women killed by a partner/ex-partner had separated or taken steps to separate — 30% killed within the first month and ~70% within the first year of separation.
- **Restraining orders:** among IPH victims who had a restraining order, about 1 in 5 were killed within two days of it being issued and about 1 in 3 within a month — protective for many, but not a physical guarantee.

### 8. Economic and health cost
- **Global cost:** ~2% of global GDP, about US$1.5 trillion (UN Women) — roughly the size of Canada's economy; the World Bank finds costs up to 3.7% of GDP in some countries; EIGE estimates gender-based violence costs the EU ~€366 billion per year. Estimates from other bodies range from ~1–2% (IMF-cited studies) up to 5% (World Economic Forum framing).
- **National examples:** IPV ~US$5.8bn/year (US, older estimate) and ~C$1.16bn (Canada); domestic violence ~£32.9bn (England & Wales); violence against women and children ~A$11.38bn/year (Australia).
- **Health consequences (meta-analysis of 201 studies, 250,599 women, 2024):** IPV is associated with markedly increased odds of depression (OR 2.04–3.14), PTSD (OR 2.15–2.66), and suicidality (OR 2.17–5.52), plus elevated alcohol use. The WHO multi-country study documented lifetime physical/sexual IPV ranging from 15% (Ethiopia province) to 71% (a Japan site) and consistent links to injury and adverse physical, mental, sexual, and reproductive health.

### 9. Data-quality caveats (visualize uncertainty honestly)
- **Coverage gaps:** WHO covered 161 countries (90% of women), but femicide reporting has collapsed to just 37 countries for 2023 (from 75 in 2020). With gender-related motive missing in ~40% of female homicides, UNODC femicide totals are conservative lower bounds.
- **Uncertainty representation differs by source:** WHO reports formal uncertainty intervals (e.g., 27%, UI 23–31%). UNODC uses "uncertainty bands" derived from imputing missing country data — the reports explicitly state these bands are **not** confidence intervals. The apparent 2023→2024 dip (51,000→50,000) reflects data discrepancies, not a genuine decline.
- **Cross-country comparisons are treacherous:** higher recorded rates often signal better data, reporting, and awareness rather than more violence (e.g., Finland 57% vs Bulgaria 12% in the EU survey; North American femicide rose 29% from 2017–2022 partly due to improved recording). Legal definitions of rape and femicide also vary.
- **Surveys undercount:** stigma, recall bias, interview safety, and definitional differences all suppress disclosure. Even the best prevalence surveys are conservative — the true scale exceeds every point estimate shown.

## Recommendations
- **Make the survey-vs-recorded gap the central visual.** Lead with WHO's 27% / 736 million (with the UI band), then cascade the reporting funnel: "<40% seek any help → <10% go to police → <5% recorded (EU survey)." Follow with the England & Wales rape funnel as a concrete "of 100 incidents" sequence: ~16 reported → recorded → ~2.7% of recorded charged → convicted. This renders the "dark figure" viscerally legible.
- **Always plot uncertainty.** Show WHO uncertainty intervals and UNODC uncertainty bands explicitly, and annotate that UNODC bands are imputation ranges, not CIs. Never present a femicide point estimate without noting the 37-country reporting base.
- **Label source, body, and year on every datapoint** — WHO 2018/2021; UNODC/UN Women 2022/2023/2024; ONS/Home Office/CPS/MoJ; ABS 2021-22; Eurostat/FRA/EIGE 2024; Kaspersky 2023; Campbell 2003. In the legend, visually distinguish survey-based prevalence from administrative/police-recorded counts.
- **Where sources disagree, show the range and say why:** cost of VAW (2% up to 3.7% of GDP; methods differ); the "once in court" conviction figure (~53% CPS completed-prosecution rate vs 58–75% jury-verdict rate — different definitions); charge rates (provisional ~2.7% rising toward ~3.9% on later revision).
- **Define benchmarks that would change the narrative:** a rising number of countries reporting femicide data (toward the 2020 level of 75); rising police-report and charge rates in national victimization surveys; and a narrowing survey-vs-recorded gap. Track these as the honest progress indicators — and note that, so far, global IPV prevalence and femicide totals have been essentially flat over a decade.

## Caveats
Figures mix reference years and publishing bodies; femicide totals are best read as lower bounds given the reporting collapse and missing-motive problem. The "1 in 3 / 736 million" and "27%" figures both come from the same 2018 WHO exercise but measure different populations (all women 15+ including non-partner sexual violence, versus ever-partnered women 15–49 for IPV) — do not conflate them. Some widely circulated reporting-gap statistics (e.g., "70–81% of domestic violence is never reported") derive from secondary or advocacy compilations rather than primary population surveys and should be flagged as such. Justice-system charge and conviction rates are provisional and revise upward over time, and CPS case-based counts are not directly comparable to ONS/Home Office offence-based counts. Stalkerware detection figures reflect only one vendor's user base and drastically understate true prevalence.
