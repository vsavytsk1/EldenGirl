# The Witch: Etymology, History, Myth, and the Long Argument About Knowledge and Power

## TL;DR
- The popular thesis the user arrived with — that "witch" always meant a woman punished for possessing misunderstood knowledge or technology — is **partly true and partly a modern romantic invention**: gendered vulnerability, and the policing of women's speech, sexuality and reproductive knowledge, were real, but the specific "persecuted midwife-healer / suppressed pagan cult" story has been dismantled by professional historians (Harley, Hutton, Roper, Purkiss, Briggs). The strongest surviving version is subtler and more interesting than the myth.
- The European witch trials (c. 1450–1750, peak 1560–1630) killed an estimated **40,000–50,000 people** out of perhaps **90,000–110,000 tried** — roughly 75–80% women overall, with striking male-majority exceptions (Iceland, Estonia, Finland, Russia, Normandy). The "nine million" figure is a debunked exaggeration originating with Matilda Joslyn Gage and spread by the Wiccan "Burning Times" narrative.
- Witch-hunting is not safely in the past: it is a living human-rights emergency across Africa, South Asia and Melanesia, prompting a 2021 UN Human Rights Council resolution — and a parallel exoneration movement (Scotland 2022, Catalonia 2022, Connecticut 2023) is retroactively clearing the accused. Arthur C. Clarke's Third Law ("any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic") captures a genuine two-way traffic between magic and science that the "titanic women" of this field — from Murray to Federici, Larner to Roper — spent their careers mapping.

## Key Findings

1. **Etymology is genuinely uncertain, and the "wise woman" reading is speculative.** Old English *wicca* (masculine) and *wicce* (feminine) derive from the verb *wiccian*, "to practice sorcery." The Oxford English Dictionary calls the origin uncertain; Anatoly Liberman writes that "none of the proposed etymologies of witch is free from phonetic or semantic difficulties." Competing derivations connect it to PIE *\*weik-* ("to consecrate/separate," linking to Latin *victima*), to *\*weg-* ("to be lively," yielding "necromancer"), or — the reading beloved of modern Wicca — to a root meaning "wise." The last is plausible but unprovable.

2. **The female form dominated, but the terms were a tangle of "poison," "knowledge," and "sacredness" across every language.** Latin *strix/striga* (screech-owl demon), *saga* (wise-woman/seer), *venefica* and *malefica* (poisoner/evildoer); Greek *pharmakis*/*pharmakon* (the drug that is both poison and remedy); the Persian *magi* that gave us "magic"; Hebrew *kashaph*. This ambiguity is the etymological engine of the whole story.

3. **"Thou shalt not suffer a witch to live" (Exodus 22:18) rests on a contested translation.** The Hebrew *mekhashepha* was rendered *pharmakos* in the Greek Septuagint and *maleficos/veneficos* in Latin — words spanning poisoner, drug-user and sorcerer. Johann Weyer argued in 1563 that the verse condemned poisoners, not witches. Modern scholarship is split: some hold "sorceress" is defensible given parallel uses in Exodus 7, but nearly all agree the King James "witch" carried freight the Hebrew did not.

4. **The scholarly consensus corrects the old myths in three big ways:** the death toll is tens of thousands (not millions); the *Malleus Maleficarum* was influential but never official Church doctrine (it was condemned by the University of Cologne's theologians); and Margaret Murray's "surviving pagan cult" thesis — the seed of Gerald Gardner's Wicca and the "Burning Times" — is rejected by mainstream historians.

5. **The "technology misunderstood" thesis survives only in modified form.** David Harley's "Historians as Demonologists" (1990) showed the midwife-witch is largely a myth — midwives were more often witnesses *for* the prosecution. Accusers were frequently women; accusations usually arose from village quarrels over food, childcare and neighborly obligation, not elite suppression of knowledge. But the flying-ointment pharmacology (tropane alkaloids) is real, and the deeper point — that women's bodies, speech and reproductive knowledge were policed — retains force.

6. **The field was built disproportionately by women**, from Gage and Murray through Ehrenreich & English, Federici, Merchant, Barstow, Larner, Karlsen, Purkiss, Roper, Pócs and Rowlands — plus Doreen Valiente, who gave modern Wicca its liturgy. Their work ranges from rigorously archival to romantically mythic, and the tension between those poles *is* the story.

## Details

### 1. Etymology and Language — the deep roots of a slippery word

The modern English "witch" descends through Middle English *wicche* from two Old English nouns: *wicca* (masculine — "sorcerer, wizard, man who practises witchcraft") and *wicce* (feminine — "female magician, sorceress"), both formed from the verb *wiccian*, "to practise witchcraft." The compound *wiccecræft* ("witchcraft," craft = power/skill) is attested in Old English, alongside *wiccedom*.

Beyond this the ground gets soft. The OED flatly calls the origin "uncertain," and the etymologist Anatoly Liberman summarises the field's frustration: "None of the proposed etymologies of witch is free from phonetic or semantic difficulties." The leading candidates:

- **Calvert Watkins** derived the nouns from Proto-Germanic *\*wikkjaz* ("necromancer," literally one who wakes the dead), from PIE *\*weg-* "to be strong, be lively."
- **Wiktionary and others** favour Proto-Germanic *\*wikkōną* (compare West Frisian *wikje* "to foretell," Low German *wicken* "to soothsay," Dutch *wichelen* "to divine") from PIE *\*weik-/\*weyk-* "to consecrate; to separate" — cognate, strikingly, with Latin *victima*, "sacrificial victim." Eric Partridge, John Ayto and Joseph Shipley all connected *witch* to *victima* and to Old High German *wihen/weihen* "to consecrate," *wih* "holy" (compare Gothic *weihs* "holy"). On this reading the word carries the aura of the sacred — "the priests of a suppressed religion naturally become magicians to its successors," as Weekley put it.
- **The "wise" derivation** (from a root related to *wit/wisdom*), promoted in Wiccan circles, is described even by sympathetic linguists as "plausible… [but] going beyond the Old English record is speculative."

Anglo-Saxon glossaries show the semantic breadth: *wicca* glosses Latin *augur*; *wicce* stands for "pythoness, divinatricem"; in the 15th-century *Three Kings of Cologne*, *wicca* even translates *Magi*. A parallel Old English word, *lyblæca* ("wizard"), is rooted in *lybb* "drug, poison, charm" — precisely mirroring the poison/magic overlap of the Hebrew and Greek terms.

**Across languages the same three ideas — harm, knowledge, and the sacred — recur:**

- **Latin:** *striga/strix* (a screech-owl demon that devoured children — the root of Italian *strega*), *saga* ("wise woman, seer"), *venefica* ("poisoner"), *malefica/maleficus* ("evildoer," the gender-neutral term used in the Latin Bible), *lamia* (child-devouring demon).
- **Greek:** *pharmakis* ("sorceress") and *pharmakon* — the untranslatable word meaning simultaneously drug, poison, and remedy. Greek also distinguished *goēteia* (low sorcery/necromancy), *theourgia* (theurgy, ritual union with the divine) and *mageia* (the arts of the Persian *magi*, the root of "magic").
- **Hebrew:** *kashaph/mekhashepha* (see §3).
- **Russian/Slavic:** *ved'ma* (witch) is transparently derived from the root "to know" (*vedat'*); the male *ved'mak/vedmak* denotes a warlock strongly associated with the shamanic side of Slavic paganism, both healer and harmer.
- **Nordic:** the *völva* (staff-bearing seeress) practised *seiðr*, a form of magic so associated with the feminine that a man who practised it incurred *ergi* — a stigma of unmanliness/passivity.
- **Basque:** *sorgin(ak)* — assistants of the goddess Mari, a word that blurs mythological beings and real religious figures; the Basque country saw Pierre de Lancre's mass trials of 1609 (Labourd).
- **Finnish/Sami:** *noita* (Finnish "witch") derives from an old Sami word for **shaman**.
- **Non-European terms often denote something quite different from the European "witch," and calling them "witch" is frequently a colonial mistranslation.** The Yoruba *àjẹ́* names a woman said to wield "myriad arcane biological, spiritual and cosmic powers"; the honorific *Iyami Aje* ("my mother") reflects the mother's elevation to the divine, and scholars such as Ayo Adeduntan argue that translating *àjẹ́* as "witch" is "especially dangerous because… the accused can be ostracized or lynched." Comparable caution applies to Akan/Ashanti *bayi*, Bantu terms (Swahili *mchawi*), the Indian *ḍākinī*, Japanese *majo*, and Mesoamerican *nahual* (shape-shifter) and *tlahuelpuchi* (blood-sucking shapeshifter). In each case European colonizers and missionaries flattened distinct indigenous categories — some benevolent, some ambivalent — into the single demonized figure of "the witch."

### 2. The Long Prehistory — from Circe to the Canon Episcopi

The witch is older than Christianity. Ancient law codes criminalised harmful magic long before the Devil entered the picture: the **Code of Hammurabi** (18th c. BCE) prescribed death (via a river ordeal) for casting an unprovable spell; the Roman **Twelve Tables** (c. 450 BCE) punished those who magically "charmed away" a neighbour's crops; and the **Lex Cornelia de sicariis et veneficis** (81 BCE) lumped together assassins and poisoners (*venefici*) — again the poison/magic overlap.

**Classical literature gave Europe its witch archetypes.** In Greek myth the witches are beautiful and dangerous and live at the edges of the world: **Circe** on her island of Aiaia, turning men to swine; **Medea** from distant Colchis; **Hecate**, goddess of crossroads and the dead; the love-sick Simaetha of Theocritus. Roman writers made them hideous: Horace's **Canidia** and Sagana; Lucan's terrifying **Erichtho**, who cut fetuses from wombs for her necromancy; Apuleius's Pamphile and Meroe. Near-Eastern and Mediterranean child-killing demons — the Semitic **Lilith**, the Greek **Lamia**, the Roman **strix** — fused with this literary tradition. As Gianfrancesco Pico della Mirandola complained in his *Strix* (1523), where antiquity had "three or four" famous witches, "today one can find six hundred Medeas in one city and twelve thousand Circes in another" — a vivid sign of how classical models were retrofitted onto contemporary women.

Crucially, early-medieval Christianity was largely **skeptical** of witchcraft's reality. The **Canon Episcopi** (c. 900, long attributed to a 4th-century council) declared that women who believed they flew at night with the goddess **Diana** were deluded by the Devil into *imagining* it — the flights were dreams, and to believe otherwise was itself a species of infidelity. The penitentials treated magic as sin and superstition. The catastrophic shift, between roughly 1400 and 1450, was from "witchcraft is a delusion" to "**witchcraft is real, organised, and Satanic**" — the invention of the diabolical pact and the sabbath. Only then did mass hunting become possible.

### 3. Exodus 22:18 and the Politics of Translation

Few sentences have killed more people than the King James Version's "Thou shalt not suffer a witch to live" (Exodus 22:18; 22:17 in the Hebrew). The underlying Hebrew word is *mekhashepha* (root *k-sh-p*). In the **Greek Septuagint** it was rendered *pharmakos* — a word denoting one who works with *pharmaka*, i.e. drugs, poisons and potions. Jerome's Latin **Vulgate** used *maleficos*/*veneficos*. The English tradition (Tyndale, then the KJV) chose "witch."

**Johann Weyer**, physician to the Duke of Cleves, argued in *De praestigiis daemonum* (1563), after consulting Hebrew, Greek and Latin editions and rabbinical commentary, that the verse condemned **poisoners**, not witches. Reginald Scot made the same case in English in 1584. The counter-argument, pressed by conservative scholars, is that *mekhashepha* is used elsewhere (Exodus 7:11, the "sorcerers" of Pharaoh's court) in contexts that plainly mean magic, not herbalism, so "sorceress" is defensible. The honest conclusion: the precise meaning of a word written millennia ago is unrecoverable, but "witch" in the loaded early-modern sense is almost certainly an anachronistic over-translation — and translation choices (the Vulgate's, then the KJV's) genuinely shaped the theology that licensed the hunts. It is telling that modern translations overwhelmingly abandon "witch": the ESV, NIV, NASB and New Living Translation all render it "sorceress."

### 4. The European Witch Trials — scale, geography, mechanism

**Scale and chronology.** The scholarly consensus, built by Brian Levack, Robin Briggs, Wolfgang Behringer and others, is that between roughly **1450 and 1750**, with the peak in **1560–1630**, there were on the order of **90,000–110,000 witch trials** and **40,000–50,000 executions**. Levack's widely used survey (*The Witch-Hunt in Early Modern Europe*) originally estimated ~60,000 executions from ~110,000 trials; in later editions he revised the execution figure down to around **45,000** (German executions from "30,000 or more" to 20,000–25,000; France from 4,000 to 3,000; Britain as few as ~1,500). Robin Briggs put it definitively in *Witches and Neighbours* (1996, p. 8): "a potent myth has become established, to the effect that 9 million women were burned as witches in Europe; gendercide rather than genocide. This is an overestimate by a factor of up to 200, for the most reasonable modern estimates suggest perhaps 100,000 trials between 1450 and 1750, with something between 40,000 and 50,000 executions, of which 20 to 25 per cent were men." So roughly a fifth to a quarter of the executed were men.

**Geography was wildly uneven.** The Holy Roman Empire (especially the German prince-bishoprics) was the killing field — perhaps 20,000–25,000 executions, roughly half the European total. Germany's worst mass panics: **Trier** (1581–1593, ~368 executed in the city, possibly ~1,000 in the diocese); **Fulda** (1603–1605, ~205 burned); **Würzburg** (1626–1631, ~157–219 in the city, ~900 in the prince-bishopric); and **Bamberg** (1626–1632, est. 300–900+), which built a dedicated *Hexenhaus* (witch prison) and produced the harrowing smuggled letter of Mayor **Johannes Junius**, who wrote to his daughter that he confessed only under unbearable torture and was "innocent." Scotland (~2,500 executed of ~3,837 accused; see §6) and parts of Switzerland were also lethal.

By contrast, the Mediterranean Inquisitions were comparatively **restrained**. The Spanish, Roman and Portuguese Inquisitions, precisely because they were centralised, professional bureaucracies, were skeptical of witchcraft confessions. The Spanish Inquisitor **Alonso de Salazar Frías**, after investigating the Basque **Zugarramurdi/Logroño** panic of 1609–1614, concluded there was no proof and instituted rules of evidence that effectively ended Spanish witch-hunting. The Roman Inquisition handed down relatively few witchcraft death sentences (out of tens of thousands of cases). Ireland had only about four executions across its entire history; the Dutch Republic stopped early.

**Sex ratios by region.** Overall ~75–80% of the accused were women (in England over 90%), but the exceptions are revealing: in **Iceland** roughly 90% of the accused were men; in **Estonia**, **Finland**, **Russia** and **Normandy**, men were a majority or near-majority. This variance is fatal to any simple "war on women" reading and is one of the strongest data points for the revisionist historians.

**Key texts:**
- ***Malleus Maleficarum*** (Heinrich Kramer, with Jacob Sprenger's name attached, 1487) — the "Hammer of Witches," which fused misogyny ("no one does more harm to the Catholic Church than midwives") with a procedural manual and went through many editions on the new printing press (eight editions by 1500, five more by 1520). Its **reputation exceeds its actual authority**: the theology faculty of the University of Cologne condemned it as recommending illegal and unethical procedures inconsistent with Catholic doctrine, Sprenger appears to have distanced himself, and Kramer had been expelled from Innsbruck by the local bishop for misconduct during a 1485 trial. It was a bestseller, not an official rulebook.
- ***Formicarius*** (Johannes Nider, c. 1435) — an earlier source for the midwife-witch trope.
- ***Daemonologie*** (King **James VI of Scotland / I of England**, 1597) — a monarch's endorsement of hunting.
- ***Cautio Criminalis*** (**Friedrich Spee**, 1631) — the Jesuit confessor's devastating attack on torture, arguing that the trial process itself manufactured guilt: since few would confess voluntarily, torture produced false confessions and fresh denunciations, feeding the panics at Trier, Würzburg and Bamberg.
- ***The Discoverie of Witchcraft*** (**Reginald Scot**, 1584) and Weyer's *De praestigiis daemonum* (1563) — the great skeptical works.

**Mechanisms.** Inquisitorial (as opposed to accusatorial) procedure, judicial torture, the diabolical pact, the nocturnal sabbath, the "witch's mark" (a supposedly insensible spot pricked with needles), swimming/"ducking" tests (the water rejected the guilty, who floated), and — in Salem — "spectral evidence" (testimony about the accused's ghostly apparition).

### 5. The "Technology Misunderstood" Thesis — tested honestly

**The strongest case for it.** Women were the principal holders of a vast body of practical knowledge: obstetrics and midwifery, herbal pharmacology, brewing, dairying, food preservation, the care of the sick and dying. The **flying-ointment** tradition is pharmacologically real: recipes recorded by Scot (1584), Weyer, della Porta and the Spanish physician **Andrés de Laguna** (1570s) list *Solanaceae* — belladonna, henbane, mandrake, datura — plus aconite (wolfsbane). These contain **tropane alkaloids** (atropine, hyoscyamine, scopolamine) that are absorbed transdermally and produce deliriant, "flight"-like and out-of-body sensations; Laguna analysed a seized ointment and identified it as a narcotic salve (*unguentum populeum*), not a diabolical agent. The professionalisation and licensing of medicine did progressively exclude women. Silvia Federici's **enclosure** argument in *Caliban and the Witch* (2004) — that the loss of common lands destroyed women's economic independence, and that "with the persecution of the folk healer, women were expropriated from a patrimony of empirical knowledge" — is a serious materialist reading. And the **alewife-to-witch** iconography (pointed hat, cauldron, broom, cat) is superficially suggestive: women dominated brewing, and Judith Bennett's *Ale, Beer, and Brewsters in England* documents their squeezing out by male guilds.

**The rigorous critiques.** Here the professional historians largely dismantle the popular story:

- **David Harley, "Historians as Demonologists: The Myth of the Midwife-Witch" (*Social History of Medicine*, 1990)**, searched the archives and found that regular midwives were *not* typically prosecuted; often they served as **expert witnesses for the prosecution** (examining accused women for the witch's mark). Harley's stinging line is that historians repeating the midwife-witch story "have behaved like demonologists." The trope traces to Murray, Thomas Forbes, and then Ehrenreich & English, not to the trial records — and, ironically, by relying on shoddy sources it re-entrenched the very image of midwives as "marginal, creepy crones dabbling in the occult."
- The **alewife thesis is largely folk-etymology.** As historians and beer writers (drawing on Bennett) note, the broom/besom and the pointed hat predate or postdate the demonizing of brewsters and were never actually documented as a direct pipeline to witch iconography; cauldrons "don't really ever actually come up" in the medieval brewster-to-witch record. It is a satisfying story with thin evidential support — even brewing historians conclude alewives "weren't called witches directly."
- **Accusers were often women**, and accusations typically grew out of **village-level quarrels** — a refused loan, a sick child, curdled butter, a boundary dispute — not top-down elite suppression of female knowledge. Robin Briggs's *Witches and Neighbours* and Lyndal Roper's work ground the hunts in local social psychology and neighbourly obligation.
- **Margaret Murray's pagan-cult thesis is rejected.** Murray argued (*The Witch-Cult in Western Europe*, 1921) the accused belonged to a surviving pre-Christian fertility religion worshipping a horned god. Ronald Hutton (*The Triumph of the Moon*, 1999; *The Witch*, 2017) shows this is unsupportable — Murray "ignored or misquoted evidence" and rationalised the impossible; Norman Cohn (*Europe's Inner Demons*, 1975) demonstrated she had distorted her sources. Gerald Gardner nonetheless built Wicca on Murray's foundation, and the modern Pagan "**Burning Times**" narrative — nine million dead in a genocidal war on the Old Religion — descends from Murray and, before her, the Romantic historian **Jules Michelet** (*La Sorcière*, 1862), who cast the witch as a peasant rebel-priestess, and the American suffragist **Matilda Joslyn Gage** (*Woman, Church and State*, 1893), who launched the nine-million figure.

**Where the thesis retains real force.** Even the revisionists concede that the hunts were **gendered**: three-quarters of victims were women, and the demonological literature obsessed over female sexuality, reproduction and speech. The figures of the "scold" and the "shrew," and the ducking-stool used against them, show a real machinery for policing women who talked too much or too sharply. The witch was, as Hutton frames her, essentially **the neighbour falsely accused of causing supernatural harm** — and the vulnerabilities that made a woman accusable (old age, poverty, widowhood, a sharp tongue, a reputation for cursing) are precisely the vulnerabilities the contemporary global data confirm (§6). So the user's instinct is not wrong so much as mis-specified: the witch hunts were less about *suppressing advanced female knowledge* than about *channeling communal fear and misogyny onto marginal women* — with knowledge, when it appeared, as aggravating rather than causal.

### 6. Global and Contemporary — witch-hunting as a living emergency

Witch-hunting never ended; it moved. Today it is a documented human-rights crisis:

- **Sub-Saharan Africa:** Ghana's "**witch camps**" in the north are the subject of Amnesty International's April 2025 report *Branded for Life*, which documented "more than 500 people—primarily women aged 50 to 90—…surviving in four informal 'witch camps'" (based on visits in November 2023 and April 2024); Amnesty senior researcher Michèle Eken stresses these accusations "are rooted in misogyny and harmful stereotypes." Ghana's Parliament's own 2021 count of five prominent camps totalled 539 inmates, 92% of them women. The lynching of 90-year-old **Akua Denteh** on 23 July 2020 at Kafaba (East Gonja Municipality, Savannah Region) — after a priestess accused her — drew global attention; two women were later jailed for manslaughter. Ghana's Parliament passed a bill in July 2023 to criminalise witchcraft accusation, but as of Amnesty's July 2025 reporting the bill **did not receive presidential assent** and there is still no such law in force. In **Tanzania**, elderly women and people with **albinism** are murdered — the latter for body parts believed to hold magical power (Mariamu Staford survived having her arms cut off in 2008). The **DRC** sees children accused as "child-witches."
- **India:** *dayan/dain* killings persist in Jharkhand, Bihar, Assam and elsewhere; several states have passed anti-witch-hunting laws.
- **Papua New Guinea:** *sanguma* (sorcery) accusations trigger torture and murder, often of women.
- **Saudi Arabia** has prosecuted "sorcery" as a capital crime; accusations also occur in **Nepal** and **Indonesia**.

The **Witchcraft and Human Rights Information Network (WHRIN)**, led by Gary Foxcroft, and Lancaster University's Dr Charlotte Baker stated on the 2021 UN resolution: "Reported data shows that in the last decade that there have been **at least 20,000 victims** of these harmful practices across **50 countries and 6 Continents** who have faced extreme human rights violations including loss of life, limbs and property." On **12 July 2021** the **UN Human Rights Council adopted, without a vote, Resolution A/HRC/RES/47/8** — "Elimination of harmful practices related to accusations of witchcraft and ritual attacks" (HPAWRA), sponsored by Cameroon on behalf of the African Group — the first UN resolution of its kind, expressing concern at killings, mutilation, trafficking and torture inflicted with impunity on women, children, older persons, people with disabilities and people with albinism.

The demographics of who gets accused today — **widows, the elderly, the disabled, the poor, and sometimes the propertied** (accusation as a land-grab) — retrospectively illuminate the European pattern: the witch is the vulnerable, dependent, or inconveniently independent neighbour.

**The exonerations and apologies movement:**
- **Scotland:** the **Witches of Scotland** campaign (launched 2020 by KC Claire Mitchell and writer Zoe Venditozzi) won a formal apology from First Minister **Nicola Sturgeon** at Holyrood on **International Women's Day, 8 March 2022**: "I am choosing to acknowledge that egregious, historic injustice and extend a formal posthumous apology to all those accused, convicted, vilified or executed under the Witchcraft Act 1563." Per the University of Edinburgh's Survey of Scottish Witchcraft, an estimated **3,837 people were accused (84% women)** and about **2,500 executed**. A follow-up pardon bill (Natalie Don MSP) was later shelved.
- **Catalonia** issued a parliamentary vindication/pardon of accused witches in 2022.
- **Connecticut** absolved its 12 convicted "witches" (11 executed) on **25 May 2023** (Senate vote 33–1), after a campaign by the CT Witch Trial Exoneration Project.
- **Massachusetts** exonerated **Elizabeth Johnson Jr.** in 2022 — the last un-cleared Salem defendant — after an eighth-grade civics class took up her cause.
- **Norway's Steilneset Memorial** (Vardø, designed by Peter Zumthor with artist Louise Bourgeois, opened 2011) commemorates the 91 people burned in the Finnmark trials.

### 7. Myth, Mysticism, and Folklore

Beneath the trials runs a deep substratum of myth. The **Wild Hunt** — a spectral cavalcade of the dead — recurs across Germanic Europe, sometimes led by female night-travelling goddesses: **Diana**, **Herodias**, **Holda/Frau Holle**, and **Perchta**, the "ladies of the night" whose nocturnal processions the Canon Episcopi tried to dismiss as dreams. In Slavic folklore the ambivalent forest-hag **Baba Yaga** lives in a hut on chicken legs. The Norse **völva** and *seiðr* magic; the Hungarian **táltos** and the South-Slavic **kresnik** (spirit-warriors who battle for the harvest); the Sicilian **donas de fuera** ("ladies from outside," fairy-cult women) — all form a pattern the historian **Carlo Ginzburg** made central.

Ginzburg's ***The Night Battles*** (*I Benandanti*, 1966) reconstructed, from Friulian Inquisition records, the **benandanti** ("good walkers") — peasants born with the caul who believed their spirits left their bodies on the Ember nights to battle witches with fennel stalks for the fertility of the crops. Over a century of interrogation the inquisitors slowly *reinterpreted* these self-described anti-witches into witches — a documented case of the sabbath stereotype being imposed from above. In ***Ecstasies*** (1989) Ginzburg pushed further, arguing for a pan-European **shamanic substrate** beneath the sabbath imagery, drawing parallels to the Livonian werewolf and the Wild Hunt. Reviews were and remain mixed — many historians find the pre-Christian-survival claim under-evidenced — but Ginzburg preserved the "kernel of truth" in Murray that Hutton and others still debate: not an organised cult, but real strands of ecstatic and visionary folk belief. Recurring cross-cultural elements — familiars, shapeshifting, the evil eye, and above all the witch as the *inverted neighbour* (doing at night the opposite of what a good community member does by day) — knit these traditions together.

**Modern esotericism** reclaimed and reinvented all of this. **Gerald Gardner** launched Wicca in 1950s England, drawing on Murray, Charles Godfrey Leland's *Aradia* (1899), Freemasonry and **Aleister Crowley**. The single most important figure in giving Wicca its actual poetry and ritual was **Doreen Valiente** (1922–1999), Gardner's High Priestess, who rewrote much of the liturgy — including the "Charge of the Goddess" — stripping out Crowley and composing enduring devotional verse; she is rightly counted among the "titanic women" of this history (Hutton was one of the few she asked to be invited to her funeral). Later currents include **Dianic Witchcraft** (Z Budapest's feminist, goddess-centred, often women-only tradition), **Starhawk's Reclaiming** tradition (*The Spiral Dance*, 1979), the broader feminist-spirituality movement (Mary Daly's *Gyn/Ecology*, 1978), and today's vast, decentralised **online witchcraft** ("WitchTok," #WitchTheVote, "witch" as political self-identification in the Polish and Latin American women's movements).

### 8. The Clarke's Law Thread — magic, technology, and the woman who knows too much

**Arthur C. Clarke's three laws** appear in his essay "Hazards of Prophecy: The Failure of Imagination," collected in *Profiles of the Future* (1962; revised 1973):
- **First Law** (1962, originally just "Clarke's Law"): "When a distinguished but elderly scientist states that something is possible, he is almost certainly right. When he states that something is impossible, he is very probably wrong."
- **Second Law:** "The only way of discovering the limits of the possible is to venture a little way past them into the impossible." (Elevated to "Second Law" by others — reportedly first in the French translation — and formalised in the 1973 revision.)
- **Third Law** (first in a 1968 letter to *Science*, added to the 1973 edition): "**Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic.**" Clarke added, "As three laws were good enough for Newton, I have modestly decided to stop there" — an apt joke, given Newton's own place on the magic/science frontier. (The idea has an antecedent in Leigh Brackett's 1942 story "The Sorcerer of Rhiannon": "Witchcraft to the ignorant… simple science to the learned.")

Clarke's law names something historians have documented richly. The boundary between "magic" and "science" is recent and porous:

- **The Yates thesis.** Frances Yates's *Giordano Bruno and the Hermetic Tradition* (1964) argued that Renaissance **Hermeticism** — the magical drive to *operate on* and master nature — helped catalyse the Scientific Revolution; she even tied Bruno's Copernicanism to Ficino's "solar magic." Hugh Trevor-Roper wrote that historians of science, "after reading it, will have to think again." The strong causal version is contested (Brian Vickers and others pushed back), but it transformed Renaissance historiography.
- **Newton the magician.** John Maynard Keynes, after poring over Newton's alchemical papers (which he acquired at a 1936 Sotheby's sale), declared in his essay "Newton, the Man" (drafted 1930s; delivered at the 1946 Royal Society tercentenary; printed 1947): "Newton was not the first of the age of reason. He was the **last of the magicians**, the last of the Babylonians and Sumerians." Charles Webster's *From Paracelsus to Newton: Magic and the Making of Modern Science* (1982) traced the continuity explicitly. **Paracelsus** (1493–1541) embodied it: his "natural magic" (*magia naturalis*) and doctrine of signatures were simultaneously proto-chemistry and occult philosophy.
- **Weber's disenchantment.** Max Weber, in his lecture "Science as a Vocation" (*Wissenschaft als Beruf*, delivered at Munich, 7 November 1917; published 1919), diagnosed modernity as "the **disenchantment of the world**" (*Entzauberung der Welt*, a phrase adapted from Schiller): "One need no longer have recourse to magical means in order to master or implore the spirits, as did the savage… Technical means and calculations perform the service."
- **Keith Thomas** documented the process empirically in *Religion and the Decline of Magic* (1971), showing how magic, astrology and religion offered early-modern English people ways to manage misfortune — magic "dominant when control of the environment is weak" — and asking why magic declined as science and technology advanced.
- **The anthropological rebuttal.** Stanley Tambiah (*Magic, Science, Religion, and the Scope of Rationality*, 1990) dismantled the evolutionary "magic → religion → science" progress model of Tylor and Frazer, presenting them as parallel "orderings of reality." Bruno Latour (*We Have Never Been Modern*, 1991 French / 1993 English) argued the modern purification of "Nature" from "Society" was always a fiction generating hybrids — hence "we have never been modern," and the Great Divide between magical "them" and scientific "us" is a difference of scale, not essence.

**The reverse of Clarke's law** is the user's real theme: new and misunderstood technologies are repeatedly **coded as witchcraft or the occult**, and women who know too much are repeatedly treated as suspect. Katharina Kepler's own son wrote her defence using "medical knowledge and common sense" to explain away the "magical" illnesses she was blamed for. The trope of the woman-who-knows-too-much runs from Circe through the accused cunning-women to modern anxieties about female expertise — a continuity the "titanic women" of witchcraft studies made it their life's work to trace.

### The titanic women of the research

The field was built, disproportionately, by women — and honoring them was part of the brief. In rough chronological order of contribution:
- **Matilda Joslyn Gage** (*Woman, Church and State*, 1893) — suffragist who first popularised the (wildly inflated) nine-million figure and framed the hunts as a war on women.
- **Margaret Murray** (*The Witch-Cult in Western Europe*, 1921) — Egyptologist whose pagan-cult thesis, now rejected, nonetheless shaped a century of popular belief and seeded modern Wicca.
- **Barbara Ehrenreich & Deirdre English** (*Witches, Midwives, and Nurses*, 1973) — a movement pamphlet that made the "persecuted women healers" argument central to feminist history (and, per Harley, over-reached).
- **Carolyn Merchant** (*The Death of Nature*, 1980) — linked the witch hunts to the mechanistic domination of nature and women in the Scientific Revolution.
- **Anne Llewellyn Barstow** (*Witchcraze*, 1994) — a passionate feminist reading, criticised by academic historians for methodology but widely read.
- **Silvia Federici** (*Caliban and the Witch*, 2004) — the Marxist-feminist "primitive accumulation / reproductive labour" thesis tying witch-hunting to the birth of capitalism.
- **Christina Larner** (*Enemies of God*, 1981) — the rigorous study of Scottish witch-hunting; coined the framing of witchcraft as a "sex-related" but not strictly "sex-specific" crime.
- **Carol Karlsen** (*The Devil in the Shape of a Woman*, 1987) — showed that accused New England women were often those who stood to inherit property, threatening male lines.
- **Lyndal Roper** (*Oedipus and the Devil*, 1994; *Witch Craze*, 2004) — psychoanalytically informed studies of German trials, emphasising fantasy, the body, and old age.
- **Diane Purkiss** (*The Witch in History*, 1996) — critiqued both the demonologists and the feminist mythmakers, insisting on the witch as a figure of story and fantasy.
- **Marina Warner**, **Deborah Willis** (*Malevolent Nurture*), **Alison Rowlands** (Rothenburg), **Éva Pócs** (Hungarian/shamanic traditions), **Marion Gibson** (early modern witchcraft writing), **Kathleen Stokker** (Norwegian folk magic), **Ulinka Rublack** (*The Astronomer and the Witch*, 2015) — and **Doreen Valiente** on the practitioner side — round out a field in which women scholars, more than in almost any other domain of history, defined the questions.

The **WITCH** activist group — **Women's International Terrorist Conspiracy from Hell** — was founded in New York on **Halloween 1968** by socialist feminists including Robin Morgan, Peggy Dobbins, Judy Duffett, Cynthia Funk and Naomi Jaffe. Their inaugural "zap" hexed Wall Street; a 1969 action targeted a Bridal Fair. As religious-studies scholar Cynthia Eller observed, "By choosing this symbol, feminists were identifying themselves with everything women were taught not to be: ugly, aggressive, independent, and malicious." WITCH was reborn (as anonymous, hooded collectives in Portland, Chicago and elsewhere) after 2016 — the direct ancestor of the contemporary political reclamation of "witch."

### The individual human stories

The abstractions matter most when attached to names:
- **Anna Göldi**, a Swiss servant beheaded at Glarus on 13 June 1782 — commonly called the last person executed for witchcraft in Europe — was almost certainly killed to silence an affair with her employer, the magistrate and physician Johann Jakob Tschudi, after she was accused of putting needles in his daughter's milk. She was officially **exonerated by the canton of Glarus in 2008**, and now has a museum and an "eternal light" memorial. Her case gave rise to the term "judicial murder."
- **Katharina Kepler**, herbalist and mother of the astronomer Johannes Kepler, was accused in 1615, chained in prison for over a year, and defended over a six-year ordeal by her son, who dismantled the prosecution with reasoned argument; she was freed in 1621 and died six months later. Ulinka Rublack's *The Astronomer and the Witch* (2015) rehabilitated her from the "hideous little woman" of Arthur Koestler's caricature.
- **Tempel Anneke** (Anna Roleffes), executed at Brunswick in 1663, is remembered because her complete trial record survives — a rare, intimate window into how a village healer's reputation curdled into a capital charge.
- **Alice Kyteler** (Kilkenny, 1324), a wealthy Irishwoman, was the first person in Ireland tried for witchcraft; she escaped, but her servant **Petronilla de Meath** was flogged and burned in her place — one of the first such burnings in Ireland/Britain.
- **Agnes Sampson**, a healer and midwife, was tortured and executed in the **North Berwick** trials (1590–91) personally overseen by James VI.
- **Merga Bien**, a wealthy, pregnant woman, was burned at **Fulda** (c. 1603) — her estate a likely motive.
- The **Pendle** trials (Lancashire, 1612) and **Torsåker** (Sweden, 1675, ~71 executed in a single day, the deadliest in Swedish history) show the range.
- In colonial New England, **Tituba**, an enslaved woman of uncertain (likely Indigenous American) origin, gave the confession that ignited **Salem** (1692), where 19 were hanged and Giles Corey pressed to death.
- **Marie Laveau**, the 19th-century Vodou practitioner of New Orleans, represents the diasporic afterlife of these traditions — reverence and rumor rather than persecution.

## Recommendations
*(For a reader deciding how to think about, teach, or write about "the witch.")*

1. **Adopt the modified thesis, not the romantic one.** State plainly: the witch hunts were gendered, misogynistic, and fueled by anxiety about women's bodies and speech — but they were *not* a coordinated elite campaign to suppress female medical/technological knowledge, and *not* the persecution of a surviving pagan religion. If you want the emotional truth of the user's framing without the factual errors, anchor it in **gendered vulnerability and the policing of women**, which the evidence fully supports.

2. **Use the corrected numbers and flag the contested ones.** Cite ~40,000–50,000 executions from ~90,000–110,000 trials, ~75–80% female overall. Explicitly retire the "nine million" figure and name its lineage (Gage → Murray → the "Burning Times"). Note the male-majority regions (Iceland, Estonia, Finland, Russia, Normandy) as the decisive counter-evidence.

3. **Read the primary revisionists directly:** Levack (survey), Briggs (*Witches and Neighbours*), Roper (*Witch Craze*), Purkiss (*The Witch in History*), Hutton (*The Witch*; *The Triumph of the Moon*), Larner (*Enemies of God*, on Scotland), Karlsen (*The Devil in the Shape of a Woman*, on New England), and Harley's midwife essay — and read the feminist-materialist tradition (Ehrenreich & English; Federici; Merchant) *critically alongside* them, not as settled history.

4. **Treat the present as the key to the past.** The contemporary data (Ghana, Tanzania, India, PNG; UN Resolution 47/8; WHRIN's ~20,000 victims/decade) is the best living laboratory for who gets accused and why — and it validates the "vulnerable neighbour" model over the "suppressed expert" model.

5. **Keep Clarke's law as a frame, not a proof.** It elegantly captures the magic/science continuum (Yates, Newton-the-magician, Weber's disenchantment, Tambiah, Latour) and the coding of misunderstood power as occult — but it is an aphorism, not evidence about historical witches.

**Benchmarks that would change these conclusions:** new archival demography substantially raising execution totals or overturning the female-majority exceptions; robust evidence (currently absent) that midwives or organised cunning-folk were systematically targeted *as* knowledge-holders; or credible documentation of an organised pre-Christian cult behind the accusations. Absent those, the modified thesis stands.

## Caveats
- **Numbers are estimates.** Records are incomplete and regionally patchy; execution totals are reconstructions, and Carlo Ginzburg has floated far higher figures (3–4 million) that most scholars reject. Treat all totals as orders of magnitude.
- **Etymology is unsettled.** The "wise woman" derivation of *witch* is attractive but unproven; I have flagged it as speculative rather than fact.
- **Source quality varies.** Some vivid claims here (flying-ointment recipes, the alewife iconography) circulate heavily in popular media with thin scholarly backing; I have marked where the academic consensus is skeptical.
- **"Witch" is often a mistranslation.** Applying the European category to Yoruba *àjẹ́*, Akan *bayi*, or Mesoamerican *nahual* distorts distinct indigenous concepts and can itself endanger people; the word should be used with care.
- **Contested scholarship remains contested.** Ginzburg's shamanic-substrate thesis, Yates's Hermeticism thesis, and Federici's capitalism thesis are all influential but disputed; I have presented them as arguments, not verdicts.
- **Living harm.** Witchcraft accusation is a present-day cause of murder and displacement; the historical material should not be treated as merely antiquarian. Note the sobering coda from Ghana: a bill to criminalise accusation passed Parliament in 2023 but, as of mid-2025, had not become law — a reminder that the story of the witch is unfinished.
